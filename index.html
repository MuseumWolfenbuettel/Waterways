5.Waterways – Wasserwege

Water and Wolfenbüttel have a lifetime connection that is both a blessing and a curse. People came to this area because the Oker offered drinking water and protection from attack. In the 16th century, with the aid of Dutch master builders, a canal system was designed that drained the marshy ground and transformed it into land suitable for building. In addition, the canals served as transport routes for boats and rafts, and the water was also used to power mills. Laying out the canals promoted trade, and the many small canals that ran through the entire town offered the best fire protection imaginable in a place with timber framed buildings. Almost every house in Wolfenbüttel had access to the water of the Oker. However, the arms of the Oker not only provided water for many uses; they also discharged wastewater. The canals developed into dangerous breeding grounds for germs. Epidemics such as the plague, typhus and cholera were no rarity. The metre-high fortress walls made it difficult to dispel the stench. Not until the 19th century, when people began to fill in the canals and remove the fortifications, did urban hygiene improve. The threat from flooding remained.
